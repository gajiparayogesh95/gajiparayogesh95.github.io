## What Value Investing Means to Me

Value investing is the practice of buying businesses at a meaningful discount to their intrinsic value — and holding them long enough for that gap to close. It is less a strategy and more a mindset: one that prizes patient analysis over reactive trading, and probabilistic thinking over certainty.

My approach is rooted in the Graham-and-Dodd tradition but informed by the evolution that Buffett and Munger brought to it — moving from purely statistical cheapness toward businesses with durable competitive advantages purchased at sensible prices.

## Core Principles

<ul class="principles-list">
  <li>
    <strong>Margin of Safety First</strong>
    Every purchase must offer a meaningful discount to my estimate of intrinsic value. This cushion protects against estimation errors, bad luck, and my own blind spots. If the math only works at the exact right price, I pass.
  </li>
  <li>
    <strong>Understand the Business Before the Stock</strong>
    I want to understand how a company makes money, why customers keep coming back, and what would have to go wrong for the business to fail — before I look at a single financial ratio. A spreadsheet without a narrative is just noise.
  </li>
  <li>
    <strong>Durable Competitive Advantages (Moats)</strong>
    A cheap business that loses money is a value trap. I prioritise companies with identifiable moats — switching costs, network effects, cost advantages, or intangible assets — that protect returns on capital over time.
  </li>
  <li>
    <strong>Long Time Horizon</strong>
    Compounding works slowly and then all at once. My default holding period is "as long as the thesis remains intact." Short-term price movements are noise; long-term business outcomes are what matter.
  </li>
  <li>
    <strong>Circle of Competence</strong>
    I only invest in businesses I can genuinely understand. Saying "I don't know" and staying out is an active, value-preserving decision. Stretching outside my circle is how permanent capital losses happen.
  </li>
  <li>
    <strong>Management Quality and Capital Allocation</strong>
    A great business can be destroyed by poor management. I look for owner-operators or management teams with long track records of rational capital allocation — who think like shareholders because they are shareholders.
  </li>
</ul>

## The Investment Process

**Screening:** I start with quantitative filters — high returns on invested capital (ROIC), low debt, consistent free cash flow generation. This narrows the universe to businesses that have already proven they can earn good returns.

**Qualitative Analysis:** Next I read the last five years of annual reports, listen to earnings calls, study competitors, and try to understand the industry structure. Porter's Five Forces is a useful lens. I want to know: why does this business earn above-average returns, and how durable is that advantage?

**Valuation:** I use a combination of discounted cash flow (with conservative assumptions and a terminal value sanity check), earnings power value, and relative multiples. No single number is "right" — the goal is a range of plausible values that tells me whether the current price offers a margin of safety.

**Position Sizing:** High-conviction ideas get larger allocations. Uncertainty — about the business, the valuation, or the macro environment — reduces position size. I do not diversify for the sake of it; a portfolio of 15–25 well-understood businesses is sufficient.

**Ongoing Monitoring:** I re-read annual reports every year and ask: has the thesis changed? Has the moat narrowed? Is management still allocating capital sensibly? If the answer to any of these changes unfavorably, I reassess regardless of current price.

## What I Avoid

- **Turnaround stories** where I'm relying on management to fix structural problems
- **Highly leveraged companies** where a bad year can wipe out equity
- **Businesses I can't value** — even if the story sounds compelling
- **Trading on macro predictions** — I have no edge forecasting interest rates or GDP
