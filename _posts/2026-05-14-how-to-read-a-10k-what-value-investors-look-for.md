The 10-K is the most important document an investor can read. It is the annual report that US-listed companies file with the SEC — a comprehensive account of the business, its financials, its risks, and management's view of the future. Every public company produces one. Most investors never read them.

This is a significant edge opportunity.

A well-read 10-K tells you more about a business than any analyst report, earnings summary, or financial news article. It takes time — a first read of an unfamiliar company might take four to six hours — but that time is the price of genuine understanding.

Here is how I approach it.

## Start at the Back: Auditor's Report and Footnotes

This sounds counterintuitive, but the back of the 10-K is where problems hide. Before reading management's optimistic narrative in the front sections, check:

**The auditor's report.** Is it a clean opinion? Any "going concern" language is a serious red flag. Any qualifications deserve investigation.

**The footnotes to the financial statements.** This is where accounting complexity, related-party transactions, off-balance-sheet obligations, and revenue recognition policies live. Footnotes are deliberately dense. That's the point. If a company is using aggressive accounting, it will be in the footnotes — described accurately but obscured by complexity.

Pay particular attention to:
- Revenue recognition policy (when does the company recognise revenue, and does it match economic reality?)
- Goodwill and impairment testing (large goodwill balances from acquisitions can mask overpayment)
- Debt covenants and maturity schedule
- Stock-based compensation (often excluded from "adjusted" earnings but a real cost)
- Pension obligations (can be significant and often understated)

## Business Description (Item 1)

Now go to the front. Item 1 describes what the business actually does. Read it carefully, but also critically — it is written by management and will emphasise strengths. Ask yourself:

- Can I explain in two sentences what this company does and how it makes money?
- Who are the customers, and why do they buy from this company rather than a competitor?
- What does the supply chain look like? Are there dependencies that could disrupt operations?
- How concentrated is the customer base? (A note will often disclose if a single customer exceeds 10% of revenue.)

**The competition section** is particularly valuable. Companies are required to describe their competitive environment. The honesty of this description — and what competitors are named or conspicuously avoided — is revealing.

## Risk Factors (Item 1A)

Risk factors are boilerplate-heavy but occasionally contain genuine insight. Rather than reading every one, I skim for:

- Risks that are company-specific (not generic "interest rates could rise" language)
- Regulatory or litigation risks with material potential impact
- Customer concentration or supplier dependency
- Any new risks that were not in the prior year's filing

A new risk factor that wasn't in last year's 10-K is always worth investigating.

## Management's Discussion and Analysis (Item 7)

The MD&A is management's explanation of the financial results. This is where the narrative meets the numbers. I look for:

**Revenue drivers.** Is growth coming from volume, price, or acquisitions? Organic volume growth is healthiest; price-driven growth can be sustainable but needs context; acquisition-driven growth requires scrutiny of the economics of each deal.

**Margin trends.** Are gross and operating margins stable, expanding, or contracting? Management will often explain margin changes in the MD&A. Cross-check their explanation against the actual numbers.

**Free cash flow vs. reported earnings.** I always calculate free cash flow myself: operating cash flow minus capital expenditures. If earnings are growing but free cash flow is flat or declining, something is worth investigating — either the business is investing heavily for future growth (potentially good) or the earnings are of poor quality (potentially bad).

**Segment reporting.** Companies with multiple business units report segment results, which are often far more revealing than consolidated figures. A struggling segment can be hidden in a consolidated income statement. Look at each segment's revenue, profit, and margins separately.

## The Income Statement: Quality Over Quantity

When I read the income statement, my primary concern is the **quality** of earnings, not just the level.

| Metric | What I'm Looking For |
|--------|----------------------|
| Gross Margin | Stability or expansion over 5+ years; consistency across cycles |
| Operating Margin | Leverage to revenue — does it expand as the business scales? |
| D&A vs. Capex | If capex consistently exceeds D&A, the business is capital-hungry |
| Interest Coverage | EBIT / interest expense; I want at least 5x in most cases |
| Tax Rate | Unusual one-time tax benefits can inflate reported earnings |

I always look at five years of income statements side-by-side. Trends that are invisible in a single year become obvious over time.

## The Balance Sheet: Financial Health

The balance sheet tells me how much financial cushion the business has and how capital-efficient it is.

**Cash and debt.** Net cash (cash minus total debt) is the most important single number. A business with net cash has optionality; one with significant net debt has fragility.

**Return on Invested Capital (ROIC).** I calculate this as operating income after tax divided by invested capital (equity plus net debt). A business that consistently earns ROIC above its cost of capital is creating value. One that earns below it is destroying it. ROIC is the single number I weight most heavily in initial screening.

**Working capital trends.** Increasing receivables or inventory relative to revenue can signal either growth (fine) or deteriorating quality (a problem). A business that collects cash before it delivers its product — think subscription software or insurance — has a structural advantage.

## The Cash Flow Statement: Where the Truth Lives

The cash flow statement is the hardest to manipulate and therefore the most reliable. I focus on:

**Operating cash flow.** This should track net income over time. Persistent divergences — net income well above operating cash flow — typically indicate aggressive accruals.

**Capital expenditure.** I separate maintenance capex (required to keep the business running) from growth capex (investment in future capacity). Companies rarely split this out explicitly, so I often have to estimate based on asset age, depreciation rates, and commentary.

**Free cash flow yield.** Free cash flow divided by market capitalisation. This is the most direct measure of what the business is generating relative to what I am paying. I want at least a 4–5% FCF yield as a starting point, higher for cyclical or lower-quality businesses.

Reading a 10-K is not a passive exercise. It's a series of questions asked against a document. The best analysts I know read 10-Ks with a pen in hand, writing down every question the document raises — and then going looking for answers.
